An indoor sauna is a construction project as much as a purchase. It occupies a
room you already have, which means the interesting questions are about the
building rather than the sauna: where the power comes from, how the space is
ventilated, what the floor is made of, and how moisture leaves the room.

The advantage is that it is always available. No crossing the garden in January,
no waiting for better weather. For most households that changes the sauna from
something used occasionally into something used weekly.

Glazing the front is the usual move. It borrows light from the room beyond and
stops a small space from feeling like a cupboard.

FAQ

Questions worth asking first

The questions worth settling before you buy — how the formats differ, what your site needs, and what the room asks of you once it is in. Anything not covered here, ask us directly.

Choosing

What is the difference between a barrel and a cube sauna?

Geometry, mostly. A barrel has curved walls, which removes cold corners and reduces the air volume that has to be heated — barrels are known for coming up to temperature quickly. A cube is a flat-roofed rectangular volume, which allows much larger glazing and a more architectural presence. A barrel feels enclosed; a cube feels open to the view.

Indoor or outdoor?

Outdoor saunas give you more space, a proper cooling area, and a stronger sense of occasion. Indoor saunas get used far more often, because there is no weather between you and the room. If frequency matters more than ceremony, build inside.

What size should I choose?

Work from how many people will realistically use it at once, then add a little. Bench length decides whether anyone can lie down, which is the single biggest comfort difference. Room volume also determines heater sizing, so size and heater are one decision, not two.

Electric or wood-burning?

Electric heaters run on a timer, need no tending, and suit indoor rooms and anywhere a flue is impractical. Wood-burning stoves take longer to bring up, need fuel and a flue, and are chosen by people who want the fire to be part of the ritual. Both need to be sized to the room.

Site and installation

What does an outdoor sauna need underneath it?

A level, stable, well-drained base. Depending on the model and your ground conditions that may be a concrete pad, a gravel base, piers or an existing deck rated for the load. We will tell you what a given model requires before you order, but the base itself is arranged locally.

What electrical work is involved?

Electric heaters generally require a dedicated circuit sized to the heater, installed by a licensed electrician and inspected where your jurisdiction requires it. This should be planned before ordering, not after delivery. We can tell you the electrical requirement for a given heater so your electrician can quote it.

Do I need a permit?

It depends entirely on your municipality, the size of the structure, whether it is permanent, and where it sits on the lot. Some jurisdictions treat a small outdoor sauna as an accessory structure needing no permit; others do not. Check with your local building department early — it can affect which model makes sense.

Living with it

How long does a sauna take to heat up?

This varies with room volume, heater output, insulation and how much glazing there is. A small, well-insulated room reaches temperature considerably faster than a large one with a full glass wall. We will give you the figure for the model you are considering rather than a general number.

How much maintenance is there?

Less than people expect. Ventilate after use so the timber dries, wipe the benches, and keep the floor clean. Heater stones are periodically restacked and eventually replaced as they break down. Outdoor cabins need their exterior finish maintained on whatever schedule the manufacturer specifies.

Can I use it in winter?

Yes — winter is arguably when a sauna is best. What matters is that the walk to and from it is manageable in the dark and the cold, which is worth thinking about when you decide where to put it.

Still have a question?

Ask it directly. We would rather answer a specific question about your space than have you work it out from a general page.
